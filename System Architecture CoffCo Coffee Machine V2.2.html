Architectural approach
How the architecture has been developed and what it represents

This document is version 2.1 of COFFCO-ARCH-001 (updated Issue 2.1 — Safety Engineering review; adds SRS 3.10–3.13 (safety provisions in SRS) to requirements allocation; updates SS-2, SS-3, SS-4, SS-6 function descriptions; adds SAFE-SHUTDOWN and FLUID-EMPTY states), updated to meet INCOSE Z8 (System Architecture) and Z9 (MBSE) requirements. It provides seven formally connected view types — logical, physical, structuring principles, options/trade, behavioural, operational, and requirements traceability — addressing the concerns of all identified stakeholders per the Z9 MBSE ontology.

The architecture is developed in two stages. The logical architecture decomposes the system into functional subsystems and defines the flows between them — entirely technology-agnostic. It answers the question: what does the system need to do, and how do those functions relate? The physical architecture then allocates those logical functions to physical elements, making explicit design decisions about how the system will be realised — without over-constraining the detail design.

Where the architecture has exposed gaps in the requirement set, new requirements have been identified and flagged. Where design decisions have been made that are not mandated by the SRS, they are recorded as architectural assumptions. Both are tracked below and will be used to mature the SRS.

Architectural assumptions and requirement gaps
Decisions made during architecture development that are not mandated by the current SRS

The following assumptions were made during architecture development. Each will either be confirmed by further customer engagement, resolved by design analysis, or converted into a new requirement. Gaps identified during architecture — where the SRS did not provide sufficient guidance — are also recorded and proposed as new requirements.

Assumption A-01
Single integrated control assembly
The brew control and monitoring functions are allocated to a single control assembly. This is not mandated by the SRS — separate control and monitoring assemblies are architecturally feasible. Assumed for simplicity and footprint efficiency within the 300mm envelope.
Assumption A-02
Front-facing user interface panel
The UI panel is assumed to be front-facing on the machine. No SRS requirement specifies orientation. Front-facing is assumed as the most intuitive configuration for a desk-mounted appliance in a hotel room context.
Assumption A-03
Unified thermal path for coffee and milk
A single thermal assembly services both americano brewing and milk frothing. Separate dedicated thermal paths are an alternative. Unified path assumed to meet the footprint and mass constraints — but cross-contamination between coffee and milk must be addressed in detail design.
Assumption A-04
Top or front-loading consumable access
Housekeeping access for consumable replenishment is assumed to be from the top or front of the machine. The SRS requires tool-free access (2.5) but does not specify orientation. This assumption drives the internal layout of the fluid store.
Assumption A-05
Universal PSU with interchangeable plug module
A single universal power supply unit (110–240V, 50/60Hz) with a physically interchangeable plug module is assumed as the mechanism for regional connector compatibility. This satisfies SRS 2.3, 2.4, and 2.6 without market-specific PSU variants.
Assumption A-06
Cable lug as primary anti-theft mechanism
A Kensington-style cable anchor lug integrated into the structural chassis is assumed as the anti-theft mechanism (SRS 1.8). The SRS requires deterrence against opportunistic removal — a cable lug satisfies this without being a locking mechanism, which would create a housekeeping burden.
Assumption A-07
Removable water reservoir
The water supply is assumed to be via a removable, user-fillable reservoir rather than a plumbed connection. No SRS requirement specifies either. A removable reservoir requires no room plumbing modification (consistent with the operating environment) but creates a housekeeping refill burden not currently quantified in the SRS.
Assumption A-08
Wireless technology selection deferred
SRS 1.9 requires wireless maintenance communication but does not specify the technology. The architecture allocates this to the control assembly but defers technology selection. Wi-Fi, Bluetooth LE, and Zigbee are all feasible candidates — selection will depend on hotel infrastructure and range requirements.
Requirement gap RG-01
Water reservoir capacity not specified
SRS 1.4 requires ≥10 brews between replenishment but does not state a water reservoir volume. At approximately 250ml per americano, 10 brews requires ≥2.5 litres. A reservoir volume requirement is needed to validate the fluid store design and confirm the footprint is achievable.
Requirement gap RG-02
Standby power state not specified
The SRS requires EU energy efficiency rating A (3.5) but does not specify a standby power limit. The machine will spend the majority of its powered life in standby. A standby power requirement is needed to support the energy efficiency rating and prevent excessive heat generation in the room.
Requirement gap RG-03
Acoustic output (noise) not specified
The primary use case is early morning in a hotel room. No noise limit is in the SRS. A brewing machine generates pump and heating noise. An acoustic output requirement is needed — particularly for the brewing cycle which occurs when guests and partners may be sleeping.
Requirement gap RG-04
Cross-contamination (coffee / milk) not addressed
The architecture assumes a unified thermal path. No SRS requirement addresses the risk of coffee contaminating the milk path or vice versa. A food safety / contamination prevention requirement is needed, particularly given the HACCP obligation (SRS 4.9).
Proposed new requirement NR-01
Water reservoir minimum capacity
The system shall incorporate a water reservoir with a minimum capacity of 2.5 litres. Derived from SRS 1.4 (≥10 brews) at 250ml per americano. Verification: Test. Priority: High.
Proposed new requirement NR-02
Standby power limit
The system shall consume no more than 0.5W in standby mode. Derived from SRS 3.5 (EU energy efficiency rating A) and EU Ecodesign Regulation standby requirements. Verification: Test. Priority: Medium.
Proposed new requirement NR-03
Acoustic output limit
The system shall not exceed 55dB(A) at 1 metre during the brew cycle. Derived from the operating context (early morning hotel room, sleeping occupants) and WHO guidelines for indoor noise. Verification: Test. Priority: Medium.
Proposed new requirement NR-04
Beverage cross-contamination prevention
The system shall prevent cross-contamination between the coffee and milk fluid pathways. Derived from SRS 4.9 (food safety / HACCP) and the unified thermal path assumption. Verification: Analysis + Inspection. Priority: High.

Architectural structuring principles
The governing principles applied to all architectural decisions — Technical Standard View

Per Z8, defining structuring principles is the first step in the architecting process. These principles are applied consistently across all architectural decisions and provide the basis for evaluating architecture options. Any decision that conflicts with a principle requires explicit justification and SDA approval.

SP-01
Safety isolation
All thermal and electrical hazards shall be physically isolated from user-accessible surfaces by dedicated structural elements. Safety shall not be a property of any single subsystem — it shall be designed into the system architecture as a whole.
SP-02
Modularity
Physical elements shall be designed as discrete, replaceable modules with defined interfaces. No element shall be so tightly coupled to another that replacement or upgrade of one requires redesign of another. This supports the 365-day maintenance-free target and future product evolution.
SP-03
Loose coupling
Interfaces between subsystems shall be defined at the logical level before physical connections are specified. Changes to one subsystem shall not propagate to others unless the interface specification is changed. This minimises the impact of design changes during development.
SP-04
Single responsibility
Each physical element shall have a clearly defined primary function. Elements that serve multiple unrelated functions are discouraged — they increase coupling, complicate testing, and make fault isolation harder. The thermal assembly does not control; the control assembly does not heat.
SP-05
Design for the operating environment
Every architectural decision shall be evaluated against the operating environment constraints: 300×300×300mm envelope, ≤10kg, hotel room power only, untrained users, 365-day maintenance-free. No decision that compromises these constraints is acceptable regardless of functional benefit.
SP-06
Requirement necessity
No architectural element shall be included that cannot be traced to at least one SRS requirement or documented architectural assumption. Architectural gold-plating is prohibited. Elements that cannot be traced shall be challenged and either justified or removed.
SP-07
Technology agnosticism at logical level
Logical subsystem definitions shall remain technology-agnostic. Technology selection occurs at the physical level only. This preserves design freedom and prevents premature commitment to implementation approaches that may be superseded during detail design.
SP-08
Minimalism
Paraphrasing Saint-Exupéry (Z8): the architecture is complete not when there is nothing left to add, but when there is nothing left to take away. Every element and interface added increases system complexity, mass, cost, and failure risk. The simplest architecture that meets all requirements is preferred.
SP-09
Assumption visibility
All architectural assumptions shall be recorded explicitly (Section 5). Assumptions are not failures — they are expected and managed. An assumption that arrives unrecorded is a risk. Every assumption shall have an owner and a resolution path before the architecture is baselined.

Behavioural view — system state and process model
How the system behaves through time — states, transitions, and sequences — Behaviour/Process View (Z8)

The behavioural view is required by Z8 to complete the architecture. It describes how the system moves through operational states over time, and the sequences of events that drive transitions. This view is essential for identifying timing constraints, fault responses, and the interactions between subsystems during operation — none of which are visible in the logical or physical views alone.

Two behavioural descriptions are provided: the system state model (what states can the system be in, and what causes transitions) and the brew cycle sequence (the detailed activity flow for the primary use case).

Figure 3 — System state model. Eight operational states with transition triggers and fault/recovery paths. Safety update (Safety Engineering Review): Two additional safety-priority states added: SAFE-SHUTDOWN (triggered by any hardware safety interlock — TCO trip, PRV activation, watchdog reset, overpressure sensor, enclosure thermistor ≥45°C) and FLUID-EMPTY (triggered by level sensor < minimum on brew command). Updated assumptions register — A-09 and NR-05
New assumption and derived requirement from operational concept analysis
Assumption A-09
Guest provides milk; machine refrigerates until frothing
The hotel guest provides their own milk (from hotel minibar or room service). The machine incorporates a refrigerated milk store that keeps the milk at food-safe temperature until it is required for frothing. This assumption is not in the URD — it was introduced during architecture development and is consistent with the design review. It generates NR-05 (milk refrigeration) and adds a Milk Refrigeration component to the Milk Frother block in the physical BDD. The refrigeration function must be verified against food safety requirements (SRS 4.9) and adds a power consumption consideration for the energy efficiency requirement (SRS 3.5).
Proposed new requirement NR-05
Milk refrigeration
The system shall maintain guest-supplied milk at a food-safe temperature of ≤4°C until it is required for frothing. Derived from Assumption A-09 and SRS 4.9 (food safety / HACCP). Verification: Test (milk temperature measured after storage period). Priority: High. Cross-reference: SRS 3.5 (energy efficiency rating A must account for refrigeration power consumption).
Assumption — Housekeeping empties waste and limescale
Consistent with existing A-04
The design review states housekeeping will empty coffee waste and limescale from the machine. This is consistent with our existing Assumption A-04 (housekeeping available for consumable restocking and cleaning) and SRS 2.5 (housekeeping interface — consumable replenishment). No new assumption required — the Drip Collector block in the physical BDD captures the waste containment function, and the activity diagram shows housekeeping removing coffee waste as a distinct flow.
Assumption — Housekeeping tops up coffee consumable
Consistent with existing A-06
The design review states housekeeping will top up the coffee consumable (they call it coffee beans). This is consistent with our existing Assumption A-06 (coffee consumable supply) and SRS 2.5 and SRS 1.4. The difference in naming (beans vs. consumable) reflects our deliberate implementation neutrality — the customer has not specified beans, and the architecture must not constrain the design to a bean-based brewing mechanism.
