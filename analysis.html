1. Architecture Trade-off Studies — TD-01 to TD-06
Six decisions made during architecture definition. Each records selected option, rejected alternatives, and DfR linkage.

Trade-off studies were used to select the implementation approach for each major architectural decision point. The selected option in each case minimises lifecycle cost, maximises maintainability, and satisfies the relevant SRS requirements. All decisions are traceable to the Through Life Support (TLS) theme.

Architecture Trade Decisions TD-06 status: deferred — wireless technology subject to hotel network survey
ID Decision Selected Rejected Options Rationale and DfR Linkage Status
TD-01 Water supply method Removable reservoir Plumbed hotel water connection — requires hotel plumber, room modification, blocked retrofit; Bottled water delivery — logistics burden, no telemetry possible No hotel infrastructure modification. User-fillable from tap. Enables water hardness calibration per property (SRS 1.6). DfR: removable reservoir is the primary descale access point and eliminates a plumbing-related failure pathway entirely. Decided
TD-02 Thermal path architecture Unified thermal assembly with fluid switching Separate coffee and milk thermal paths — doubled component count, doubled seal failure surface area, doubled Fan et al. failure modes; Single-path sequential use — guest waits for thermal flush between drinks Single assembly reduces failure mode count by 40%. Assembly-swap maintenance model becomes viable at 4 kg. DfR: fewer seals, fewer joints, fewer PRV interaction points. Fan et al.: seal aging is the primary fluid path failure driver — minimising seal count directly reduces failure frequency. Decided
TD-03 Regional power connectors Universal PSU + interchangeable plug module Market-specific PSU variants — 3× PSU SKUs, 3× certification programmes, regional stocking complexity; Hardwired regional cable — non-serviceable in field, full PSU swap on plug damage Single PSU certified once (IEC 60335-1). Plug module swap in field in under 2 minutes. Regulatory change in one market (e.g. UK BS 1363 revision) requires only plug module update, not PSU re-certification. DfR: field-replaceable plug module is lowest-cost LRU in the support model. Decided
TD-04 Control system integration Single integrated Control Assembly (SS-2 + SS-5) Separate brewing MCU and UI MCU — 2× firmware images, inter-MCU comms protocol required, 2× watchdog configurations, debugging complexity doubles; Cloud-dependent control — unacceptable latency for real-time thermal loop Single MCU simplifies firmware watchdog (SRS 3.12) — one heartbeat, one reset domain, one failure mode. UI and brew control share the same state machine, eliminating inter-processor state synchronisation as a failure pathway. DfR: integration reduces component count and eliminates an inter-board connector (a known reliability weak point). Decided
TD-05 Anti-theft mechanism Anti-tamper cable lug, tool-free removal at end of life Bolted security plate — requires drill for removal, damages hotel desk, end-of-life disassembly requires tools; Hotel room lock — not feasible for all room configurations; No security — opportunistic theft confirmed risk at CONOPS Cable lug prevents opportunistic grab-and-go (SRS 1.8, <60 s without tools). Tool-free removal at end of life is a deliberate Design for Disposal decision — one of two disposal-enabling design features alongside the modular assembly architecture (SP-02). Decided
TD-06 Wireless technology Deferred Wi-Fi (2.4/5 GHz) — dependent on hotel network VLAN approval (VS IT sign-off required); Zigbee/Z-Wave mesh — requires gateway hardware at each property; Cellular (LTE-M) — SIM provisioning per machine, recurring cost Selection depends on Vacation Stables IT policy and hotel network survey results. Critical path item for telemetry (SRS 1.9) and OTA firmware (SRS 1.11). Decision gates the cloud platform go-live. SRS 1.9 and 1.11 are technology-neutral — they specify the interface, not the implementation. DfR: decision deferred to preserve design freedom until hotel network constraints are known. Deferred
2. FMECA — Failure Mode, Effects and Criticality Analysis
FM-01 to FM-09 · initial and residual RPN · causes column enriched with Fan et al. (2009) evidence at V1.1

A qualitative FMECA was conducted across all six subsystems. Failure modes are defined at the CoffCo assembly level. Risk Priority Number (RPN) = Severity × Occurrence × Detectability. All residual RPNs are ≤4 following the design controls identified. At V1.1, the causes column was enriched with component-level evidence from Fan Jinjuan et al. (2009) Engineering Failure Analysis 16:1948–1954.

Fan et al. (2009) integration: The causes column at V1.1 incorporates external failure analysis evidence for FM-01, FM-02, FM-03, FM-05, FM-08, and FM-09. Failure mode definitions, effects, RPNs, and controls are unchanged — the evidence strengthens the analytical basis without altering the risk picture.

3. Through Life Support — Analysis Conclusions
How the trade-off studies and FMECA findings feed back into design decisions
DfR Linkage Table
Design Decision DfR Benefit Failure Mode Addressed SRS Requirement
Assembly-swap maintenance model MTTR ≤4 h. Failed assembly returned to depot for refurbishment. Hotel room never opened to component level. Prevents introduction of secondary failures during field repair. FM-01FM-02FM-05 SRS 3.3 SRS 3.9
Annual FKM seal replacement (PM-03) Fan et al.: seal aging is the primary fluid path failure driver across pump, check valve, NTC holder, and heater tube connections. Scheduled replacement before end of seal life eliminates this failure pathway entirely. FM-02 SRS 3.3 IPS PM-03
Firmware descale scheduling (SRS 1.6) Scale is the root cause of pump clogging (#1 failure mode, Fan et al.), PRV spring erosion (#2), and heater tube blockage (#6). Proactive descale cycle triggered by brew count removes scale before it reaches a failure threshold. DESCALE-REQUIRED alert gives ≥10 brews warning. FM-08 SRS 1.6 SRS 1.9
Hardware TCO independent of firmware If NTC sensor fails (Fan et al. #4 failure) or firmware hangs, the bi-metallic TCO provides an independent hardware-layer cutoff. Three-layer protection: NTC firmware → thermostat → TCO. All three must fail simultaneously for heater runaway to reach the user. FM-01FM-06 SRS 3.10 SRS 3.12
PRV vented to drip tray (SRS 3.13) Fan et al. identified PRV venting inside chassis as a hidden design hazard in the tested machine — steam directed toward user and electrical components. CoffCo routes PRV vent to drip tray only. Validated by Fan et al. FTA: PRV error is a basic event in the steam leakage top event. FM-08 SRS 3.13
NTC holder upgrade PP→PPS (OA-IPS-01) Fan et al. simulation: PP NTC holder perforates at ~100°C due to manufacturing quality deficiency. PPS (the material used for the lid and chassis) operates safely at 220–240°C. Upgrade eliminates the manufacturing-quality failure pathway for this component. FM-01FM-02 OA-IPS-01 (SDD V1.2 action)
